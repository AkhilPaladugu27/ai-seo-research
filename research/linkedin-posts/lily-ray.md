## Post 1 — What the HCU Actually Penalized

**Date:** 2024  
**Theme:** Helpful Content Update impact on AI-generated content

> A lot of sites think they got hit by the Helpful Content Update because of AI content.
> 
> That's not what Google targeted.
> 
> What Google penalized: content created primarily for search engines, not users.
> 
> The distinction matters. You can use AI to create genuinely useful content.
> You cannot use AI to create content that technically answers a query but doesn't help anyone.
> 
> The sites that survived:
> → Had demonstrable expertise (author pages, credentials, first-hand experience)
> → Covered topics they actually had authority in
> → Answered questions at a depth their audience actually needed
> 
> AI is a tool. E-E-A-T is the standard. Those are two separate things.

**Key insight:** HCU targeted user-intent mismatch, not AI tools per se. AI content can rank if E-E-A-T signals are present and content is genuinely useful.

## Post 2 — AI Overviews: Which Content Gets Cited

**Date:** 2024  
**Theme:** Research on AI Overviews source selection

> We analyzed 500+ AI Overview citations across 10 topic clusters.
> 
> What Google cites in AI Overviews:
> → Content from sites with established topical authority
> → Pages with clear author attribution
> → Sources with external citations/backlinks
> → Content that matches the specific intent of the query
> → Structured content (how-to, lists, definitions) over long-form essays
> 
> What doesn't get cited:
> → Thin content, even from high-DA sites
> → Content that broadly covers a topic without specificity
> → Pages with no clear author or publisher identity
> 
> The pattern: Google's AI Overviews behave like a researcher looking for primary sources, not a reader looking for overview content.

**Key insight:** AI Overviews prefer primary-source, structured, topically authoritative content with clear attribution. Broad overview content rarely gets cited.

## Post 3 — E-E-A-T Is Not a Mystery

**Date:** 2025  
**Theme:** Practical E-E-A-T implementation

> Stop treating E-E-A-T like it's unknowable.
> 
> Here's what it actually means for content production:
> 
> **Experience:** The author has done the thing they're writing about.
> (Not "our team of experts." An actual named person with a track record.)
> 
> **Expertise:** They have credentials, history, or demonstrated knowledge in this area.
> (Linked author bio, LinkedIn profile, published work elsewhere.)
> 
> **Authoritativeness:** Other credible sources reference this author or site.
> (Backlinks, mentions, citations in other authoritative content.)
> 
> **Trustworthiness:** The site has clear ownership, contact info, editorial standards.
> 
> You can build this systematically. It just takes time and discipline.
> Most AI content operations skip it entirely. That's the actual problem.

**Key insight:** E-E-A-T is operational, not abstract — requires named authors, credentials, external citations, and site trust signals. Most AI content factories skip all four.
